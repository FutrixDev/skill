# Japanese Manga Visual Language

## Page Rhythm

- Use an establishing panel when a new scene begins.
- Use a reaction close-up before or after emotional turns.
- Use silent panels to slow time.
- Use larger panels for reveals, attacks, confessions, and irreversible choices.
- End pages with a question, surprise, action beat, or emotional hook.
- Vary panel size and shape according to drama. Do not default to equal-height horizontal strips.

## Dramatic Page Layout

- Mix one large anchor panel with smaller reaction or insert panels.
- Use tall narrow panels for pursuit, distance, isolation, or a character turning away.
- Use small inset panels for eyes, hands, feet, bowls, door handles, letters, or clues.
- Use diagonal or broken panel borders for urgency, chase scenes, shock, or spiritual tension.
- Let important figures, hands, faces, weapons, props, clothing, hair, light, aura, smoke, water, speed lines, or sound effects break panel borders when it increases drama.
- Prefer expressive manga frames over rigid rectangles: angled edges, uneven gutters, overlapping panels, interrupted borders, and borderless impact areas are all acceptable when the reading path remains clear.
- Use gutters intentionally: wide gutters for silence, tight gutters for pressure.
- A calm page can still be cinematic: one wide establishing panel, one close-up insert, one medium reaction panel, and one large emotional beat.
- Avoid pages that are just three or four full-width rectangles stacked from top to bottom unless the user asks for a simple strip layout.
- Avoid pages where every frame is a perfectly straight rectangular box unless the scene intentionally needs restraint.

## Panel Types

- Establishing wide shot: location, weather, time, mood.
- Medium dialogue shot: relationship and body language.
- Close-up: eyes, hands, mouth, object, phone screen, letter.
- Insert panel: important prop or clue.
- Action panel: diagonal layout, speed lines, motion blur, impact shape.
- Silent mood panel: sky, rain, hallway, empty chair, flickering light.

## Facial Acting

- Every visible named character should have a readable expression in the storyboard and prompt.
- Describe eyes, mouth, brows, and body language, not just emotion labels.
- Use eye close-ups, mouth close-ups, hand pressure, shoulder posture, and breath to show restrained emotion.
- Let different characters react differently in the same panel: one looks away, one clenches teeth, one forces a smile, one freezes.
- Avoid repeated neutral faces across a page unless numbness or formal restraint is the point.
- If the story calls for calm, show calm through specific acting: steady gaze, controlled mouth, lowered shoulders, or still hands.

## Color Manga Treatment

- Keep line art clean and readable.
- Use anime-style cel shading with controlled gradients.
- Keep recurring characters on fixed palettes.
- Each scene gets a stable light source and color temperature.
- Use page-level color mood: warm comfort, cold suspense, saturated action, pale memory.

## Readability

- Top-to-bottom page movement should be clear.
- Row direction must be consistent across the whole comic.
- Do not overcrowd a page with too many equal-weight panels.
- Speech bubbles should not cover faces, hands, key props, or action lines.
- Speech bubbles, narration boxes, and sound effects should feel drawn into the manga page, not pasted on afterward.
- Keep lettering short, readable, and placed with natural manga rhythm.
- Narration boxes should match the visual world: aged paper for historical scenes, ink-edged cards for wuxia/fantasy, translucent dark boxes for night scenes, soft cream manga boxes for quiet modern scenes.
- Avoid plain white rectangular caption cards that look like labels placed on top of the artwork.
